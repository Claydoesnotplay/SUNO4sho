# Genre-Specific Pacing Adjectives - Definitions

## Electronic/Dance

**pulsing** - Characterized by steady, repeating rhythmic emphasis that throbs like a heartbeat, creating hypnotic drive.

**four-on-the-floor** - Featuring a bass drum hit on every quarter note (beats 1, 2, 3, 4), the foundational rhythm of house, techno, and disco.

**breakbeat** - Built on sampled or synthesized drum breaks featuring syncopated patterns, typically from funk or jazz records.

**shuffled** - Using swing or shuffle timing where subdivisions are played with uneven, triplet-based feel rather than straight eighths or sixteenths.

**swung** - Employing a swing feel where alternate notes are played longer and shorter in a loping rhythm, common in jazz but also electronic genres.

**half-time** - Rhythm section playing at half the perceived tempo while other elements maintain original speed, creating heavy, slowed effect.

**double-time** - Rhythm section playing at twice the perceived tempo, creating urgent, accelerated feeling while harmonic progression stays constant.

**sidechained** - Rhythmic pumping or ducking effect where one element (usually kick drum) triggers volume reduction in other elements, creating breathing quality.

**drop-heavy** - Structured around climactic moments where full rhythmic and harmonic elements enter after build-up and tension.

**build-up-driven** - Organized around gradual accumulation of energy, layers, and tension leading to release or drop.

**stuttering** - Featuring rapid repetition or fragmentation of sounds, creating chopped, glitchy rhythmic effect.

## Hip-Hop/Rap

**boom-bap** - Classic hip-hop rhythm emphasizing hard kick ("boom") and snare ("bap"), typically sampled from vinyl breaks.

**trap-influenced** - Featuring rapid hi-hat rolls, snappy snares, deep 808 bass, and rhythms derived from Southern trap production.

**triplet-based** - Built on triplet subdivisions rather than straight eighths or sixteenths, creating rolling, cascading flow.

**laid-back** - Relaxed timing where rhythm sits slightly behind the beat, creating ease and groove rather than urgency.

**pocket-oriented** - Focused on finding and maintaining a deep groove pocket where all elements lock together rhythmically.

**off-kilter** - Deliberately unbalanced or asymmetrical rhythmic placement creating unstable or quirky feel.

**snare-heavy** - Emphasizing snare drum prominence in mix and arrangement, often with hard-hitting or distinctive snare sounds.

**bouncing** - Spring-like rhythmic quality with elastic rebound, creating head-nodding momentum.

**lurching** - Uneven, stumbling rhythm that moves in irregular jolts rather than smooth flow.

**chopped** - Featuring heavily edited, fragmented samples or sounds cut into pieces and rearranged rhythmically.

## Jazz

**swinging** - Employing traditional jazz swing feel with triplet-based eighth notes creating loping, bouncing rhythm.

**bebop-paced** - Fast tempo with complex, rapidly changing harmonies and virtuosic melodic lines characteristic of bebop style.

**walking** - Featuring walking bass line with steady quarter notes that outline chord changes, fundamental to jazz rhythm.

**stride** - Piano style alternating bass notes on beats 1 and 3 with chords on 2 and 4, creating bouncing, striding motion.

**brushed** - Using drum brushes rather than sticks, creating softer, swishing textures with subtler attack.

**conversational** - Interactive, call-and-response quality between instruments suggesting musical dialogue.

**improvised-feeling** - Maintaining spontaneous, in-the-moment quality even if written or rehearsed.

**breath-paced** - Following natural breathing rhythms, often in phrasing and pauses, creating organic timing.

**anticipatory** - Notes or chords arriving slightly ahead of the beat, creating forward-leaning urgency.

**delayed** - Notes arriving slightly behind the beat, creating laid-back or dragging feel.

## Rock/Metal

**galloping** - Rapid, horse-gallop-like rhythm pattern often using triplet or sixteenth note bass drum patterns (associated with NWOBHM).

**blast-beat-driven** - Extreme metal drumming technique with simultaneous rapid bass drum and snare hits, often at very high tempos.

**groovy** - Emphasizing infectious, rhythmically compelling groove that invites physical movement.

**power-chord-driven** - Built on distorted two or three-note power chords providing harmonic foundation.

**chugging** - Rhythmic, muted palm-muted guitar technique creating percussive, locomotive-like rhythm.

**breakdown-heavy** - Featuring frequent breakdowns where tempo slows and rhythm simplifies into heavy, syncopated patterns.

**palm-muted** - Guitar technique where palm rests on strings near bridge, creating tight, percussive, muted tone.

**riff-based** - Structured around memorable, repeating guitar riffs as primary compositional element.

**headbanging** - Mid-tempo rhythm and emphasis pattern conducive to headbanging movement, typically around 120-140 BPM.

**thunderous** - Massively heavy and loud with powerful low-end presence suggesting thunder.

## Ambient/Experimental

**glacial** - Extremely slow-moving like glacial ice, with nearly imperceptible changes over extended time.

**crawling** - Moving forward at barely perceptible pace, slowly creeping along.

**imperceptible** - Changes occurring so slowly or subtly they're difficult to consciously detect in real-time.

**drone-like** - Sustained, continuous tones or textures with minimal rhythmic definition or development.

**evolving** - Gradually transforming over time through subtle morphing rather than distinct events.

**patient** - Allowing ideas to unfold slowly without rushing, giving space and time for contemplation.

**unfurling** - Slowly opening or revealing itself like a flower blooming or sail unfurling.

**static** - Maintaining relatively unchanging state, not developing or progressing significantly.

**suspended-time** - Creating sense that normal time flow is halted, frozen, or stretched beyond normal perception.

**timeless** - Seemingly existing outside normal temporal flow, without clear beginning, middle, or end.

## Classical/Contemporary Classical

**andante** - Moderately slow walking tempo, typically around 76-108 BPM, meaning "at a walking pace."

**allegro** - Fast, quick, and bright tempo, typically 120-168 BPM, conveying cheerful character.

**adagio** - Slow tempo at leisure, typically 66-76 BPM, meaning "at ease."

**presto** - Very fast tempo, typically 168-200 BPM, among the fastest standard tempo markings.

**largo** - Broadly slow tempo, typically 40-60 BPM, conveying spaciousness and weight.

**vivace** - Lively and fast tempo, typically 156-176 BPM, full of life and vigor.

**moderato** - Moderate tempo, typically 108-120 BPM, neither fast nor slow.

**maestoso** - Majestic tempo and character, dignified and stately, often moderately slow.

**agitato** - Agitated character with restless, disturbed quality regardless of tempo.

**cantabile** - In singing style, emphasizing lyrical, flowing melodic character.

## Folk/Acoustic

**rambling** - Wandering, unhurried pace suggesting leisurely journey or storytelling.

**narrative-paced** - Tempo and rhythm serving story-telling function, adapting to lyrical content and phrasing.

**verse-driven** - Organized around verse structure and timing, following song form and lyrics.

**fingerpicked** - Using fingerpicking guitar technique creating polyrhythmic, flowing patterns.

**strummed** - Using strumming technique creating unified, chord-emphasizing rhythm.

**waltz-time** - In 3/4 time signature creating circular, lilting dance rhythm (strong-weak-weak).

**jig-like** - Quick, lively rhythm in compound meter (6/8 or 9/8), characteristic of Irish/Celtic jigs.

**ballad-tempo** - Slow to moderate tempo appropriate for narrative ballad, allowing clear lyrical delivery.

**march-like** - Steady, regular rhythm suitable for marching, typically in 2/4 or 4/4 with strong beat emphasis.

**campfire-paced** - Relaxed, communal tempo suitable for informal group singing and playing.

## Funk/R&B

**syncopated-groove** - Emphasizing off-beat accents and rhythmic displacement creating funky pocket.

**pocket-locked** - Tightly interlocked rhythmic relationship between instruments creating deep groove.

**one-drop** - Reggae-derived rhythm where emphasis is on beat 3, with beat 1 often omitted or de-emphasized.

**skip-beat** - Rhythm that deliberately skips expected beats creating syncopated, bouncing feel.

**ghost-noted** - Featuring quiet, lightly struck notes (especially on snare) that add texture without full accent.

**slapped** - Bass technique using thumb slap and finger pop creating percussive, funky attack.

**slinky** - Smooth, sinuous rhythm that slides and curves with sensual quality.

**serpentine** - Winding, snake-like rhythmic movement with curves and undulations.

**undulating** - Wave-like rhythmic motion with smooth rises and falls.

**head-nodding** - Mid-tempo groove that naturally induces head-nodding, typically around 85-100 BPM.

## Punk/Hardcore

**breakneck** - Dangerously fast tempo pushing limits of playability and control.

**mosh-pit-inducing** - Aggressive rhythm and tempo conducive to moshing, typically fast and chaotic.

**furious** - Extremely fast and aggressive with barely controlled rage.

**charging** - Rushing forward with aggressive momentum like a charge into battle.

**thrashing** - Violent, aggressive rhythm suggesting thrashing movement, typically very fast.

**skate-punk-paced** - Quick, driving tempo associated with skateboarding culture, around 170-200 BPM.

**rapid-fire** - Extremely fast succession of notes or rhythmic hits like automatic gunfire.

**slamming** - Heavy, aggressive emphasis creating forceful impact.

**circle-pit-ready** - Mid-tempo, heavy rhythm conducive to circular moshing patterns.

**blitzing** - Attacking with overwhelming speed and force like a blitz.

## Reggae/Dub

**one-drop** - Fundamental reggae rhythm with emphasis on beat 3, often with kick and snare together.

**steppers** - Four-on-the-floor reggae rhythm variation with kick on every beat, more driving than one-drop.

**rockers** - Reggae rhythm with kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, more rock-influenced.

**skank-rhythm** - Characteristic reggae offbeat guitar or keyboard stabs on the "and" of each beat.

**dubbed-out** - Heavily processed with dub production techniques including echo, reverb, and effects.

**echo-delayed** - Featuring prominent delay/echo effects creating rhythmic repetitions and space.

**reverb-soaked** - Drenched in reverb creating deep spatial dimension and atmosphere.

**bass-emphasized** - Foregrounding deep, heavy bass lines as primary rhythmic and harmonic element.

**offbeat-accented** - Emphasizing upbeats or offbeats rather than downbeats, creating characteristic lilt.

**space-heavy** - Generous use of silence and space between elements, allowing bass and drums to breathe.
